What is MRP? (Material Requirements Planning Explained)

A Simple Guide for Manufacturers Using Spreadsheets or Manual Planning

Most manufacturers are already doing MRP — they just don't call it that. If you're using spreadsheets, whiteboards, or manual processes to plan materials, track stock, and manage production, then you are doing Material Requirements Planning. The challenge is doing it accurately, consistently, and at scale.

What Does MRP Stand For?

MRP stands for Material Requirements Planning.

It is a system used to determine:

  • What materials are needed
  • How much is required
  • When they are needed

The goal: ensure materials are available for production — without overstocking.

How MRP Works (Simple Explanation)

At its core, MRP connects three key elements.

1

Demand

What needs to be produced — from sales orders and forecasts.

2

Bill of Materials

What components and raw materials are required, and in what quantities.

3

Inventory

What you already have in stock — determining what still needs to be purchased.

Material shortages

Purchase requirements

Production priorities

MRP combines demand, BOM, and inventory to calculate these outputs automatically.

Example of MRP in Practice

Imagine you receive an order for 100 units of a product. Your system checks:

  • The bill of materials for that product
  • Your current inventory levels

It then determines:

  • What materials you already have
  • What needs to be purchased
  • When those materials are required

This ensures production can run without delays.

Why MRP is Important in Manufacturing

Without MRP, manufacturers face:

  • Stock shortages that stop production
  • Excess inventory tying up cash
  • Poor visibility of requirements
  • Constant manual planning

MRP helps solve these by:

  • Improving planning accuracy
  • Reducing waste and excess stock
  • Ensuring materials arrive on time

The Problem: MRP in Spreadsheets

Many businesses manage MRP using Excel. While this works initially, it becomes difficult as complexity increases.

Common issues include:

What is the Difference Between MRP and ERP?

MRP focuses specifically on materials planning.

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is a broader system that includes:

  • MRP
  • Production scheduling
  • Inventory management
  • Purchasing
  • Sales and order management
  • Shop floor data capture

In modern manufacturing, MRP is usually part of a wider ERP system.

What industries use MRP?

Is MRP the same as ERP?

No. MRP focuses on materials planning, while ERP includes MRP plus other business processes in one system.
